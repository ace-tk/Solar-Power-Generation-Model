# Demand Response and Load Shifting

## Time-of-use tariff design
Time-of-use tariffs with a 3x to 5x ratio between peak and off-peak rates motivate consumers to shift flexible loads into midday solar surplus periods. Effective shift is 5-15 percent of residential and 10-25 percent of commercial demand. [Source: LBNL Demand Response Review]

## Flexible loads suitable for solar-hour shifting
Water heating, HVAC precooling, EV charging, irrigation pumping, and industrial batch processes are good candidates for shifting into midday high-solar hours. Automated controls achieve 2-4x higher shift than manual programs. [Source: IEA Energy Efficiency 2022]

## EV charging alignment
Workplace EV charging naturally aligns with midday solar. Managed charging programs can defer 60-80 percent of daytime EV load to align with local solar output. [Source: Rocky Mountain Institute EV Charging]

## Industrial flexibility
Industries such as aluminum smelting, chlor-alkali, and data centers can modulate 5-20 percent of load on sub-hourly timescales. Bilateral demand response contracts monetize this without impacting production. [Source: ACEEE Industrial Demand Response]

## Forecast-informed demand signals
Publishing day-ahead solar forecasts to demand-response aggregators 24 hours in advance improves participation rates by 30-50 percent versus same-day dispatch signals. [Source: PJM Demand Response Whitepaper]

## Building thermal mass as storage
Precooling commercial buildings by 1-2 degrees Celsius during high-solar midday hours shifts 20-40 percent of afternoon cooling load off the grid without comfort impact. [Source: ASHRAE Journal 2021]
