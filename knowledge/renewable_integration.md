# Renewable Integration Best Practices

## Geographic and technology diversification
Combining solar from multiple sites 50-100 km apart smooths aggregate output by 20-40 percent versus a single site, because cloud transients rarely correlate across that distance. Pairing solar with wind further reduces variability. [Source: IEA Wind Task 36]

## Interconnection requirements
Grid codes for utility-scale solar require low-voltage ride-through (LVRT), reactive power control, and remote telemetry. Plants above 5 MW typically must provide 15-minute real-time output telemetry to the system operator. [Source: FERC Order 842]

## Hosting capacity analysis
Feeders have a solar hosting capacity determined by thermal limits, voltage rise, and protection coordination. Exceeding hosting capacity without mitigation causes voltage excursions above 1.05 per unit. Advanced inverter functions (volt-var, volt-watt) can raise hosting capacity by 30-60 percent. [Source: EPRI DER Hosting]

## Flexibility options ranking
The IEA ranks flexibility options by cost: demand response (lowest) < storage (short duration) < dispatchable thermal (mid duration) < long-duration storage and hydrogen (highest). Deploy in that order for least-cost integration. [Source: IEA World Energy Outlook 2023]

## Curtailment vs storage economics
Below roughly 15 percent annual curtailment, economic curtailment is typically cheaper than adding storage. Above 15 percent, storage is more cost-effective. [Source: NREL Storage Futures Study]
